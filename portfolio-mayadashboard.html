Save water, save money, save the envrionment. All with responsive web.

Introduction

This project took place in the fall of 2019 as the final assignment of my Interactive design studio at Carnegie Mellon University and lasted approx 6 weeks. The project team consisted of Paul Myers, Christine Kim, and myself - all working together on the problem. Our domain that we were tasked to solve was "waste" in general, but with all of us being avid conservationists, we decided to focus on a problem that we felt was increasingly more concerning: water waste.

Our solution was a responsive web dashboard for residential water clients and service providers. It assists residential users by contextualizing their water usage through light data visualization, by giving them to tools to compare their usage to other similar users to understand what is "normal" water usage, and empowers them to make meaningful water usage decisions through goal-setting and water conservation methods. It assist water service providers by giving them the power to better assist their customers during site visits and on service calls, by providing visibility into potential water distribution infrastructure issues through a smart water map, and helps service providers better understand customer behaviors - both in terms of water usage and engagement with the utility.

Problem

Water is an essential element of human life and our modern home. An existence without water is unimaginable - how could we cook, clean, or even hydrate ourselves. While the thought of water scarcity is unimaginable here in Pittsburgh - it is becoming increasingly common around the world due to drought, pollution, climate change, and mismanagement. Cities like Pittsburgh cannot take their water supply for granted and that is why it is a problem space focusing on.

Scope: business, provider, customer

To best create a solution to tackle water waste - we felt that cities were the right scope to focus on. This is because cities typically hold the budget allocation for water/sustainability efforts, providers only focus on serving city inhabitants, and citizens (of the city) are the only customers. This overlap means that cities, providers, and customers have very similar wants: lower cost/overhead and reduce unnecessary use.

Think-Aloud Study

Now that we better understand the problem space, we moved into seeing if a current solution existed on the market that could be leveraged to solve our problem. After searching we agreed as a team that yelp offered some functionality that could potentially solve our stakeholders need for engagement while creating a more holistic public art experience in PGH. We developed a script and multiple actions for users to preform including finding public art, learning about it, creating a list of art to see, and sharing it with their friends. Findings were inconclusive on what features users wanted but in terms of usability - it seemed yelp struggled at helping with way-finding, delivering factual information, and in the realm of identity.

Storyboarding and Speed-dating concepts

Reflecting on our problem space and the TAP study - we looked for areas to ideate on that could solve our core stakeholder need of improving engagement and also improve the users experience through identity, understanding, and interaction. With that in mind we created 12 different story boards to use during speed dating sessions. The storyboards were divided into 4 sections: identity, information, increasing awareness, and way-finding. I created storyboards for identity and then worked with Alice to preform our speed dating session at CMU over the course of a couple of hours. Identity ideas - specifically a AR solution which incorporated users leaving notes and messages to the art - was the most popular.

Experience Prototyping

With the direction decided through the storyboarding process - we formulated a way to test the concept and conduct an experience prototype session. Our idea was to create an artificial piece of public art and place it in a popular square in Pittsburgh. Then users could interact with our clear plastic screens representing the AR mode on a phone. Users then could choose to either leave a message in AR space, place an emoji on the art piece, or just browse what other people said about the work. Users responded positively to the testing but what surprised the most was that users started dialogues with each other. As well as used the space to form empathetic views with others. We conducted an exit interview after users interacted with the system and we found that a majority of people felt more connected to the art and their local area through the experience.

Solution - Make Art your own!

We propose an augmented reality (AR) platform that encourages interaction between a user, the art piece and the wider community. In the AR space, users can interact with public art by posting comments, adding emojis and viewing other community members’ posts. This is reinforced by placing a sign by the physical art to inform the public of the potential for interaction. To track the level of engagement, we will measure the number of posts and active users in the app. With user-generated data, the cost of public art can be justified by analyzing the posts and quantifying the success of individual art pieces. We will partner with corporations and social media platforms for advertising and leveraging existing technology.

Users first will be prompted to download/ open the app by signage near the art. Then they will use the qr code to enter the "space" and be able to view the art piece comments through their screen

Users then can use emojis or messages to decorate, comment on, or just leave notes to the area for others to read
